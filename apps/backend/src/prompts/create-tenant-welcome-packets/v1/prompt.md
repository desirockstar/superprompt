# CONTEXT:
Adopt the role of tenant experience architect. A new tenant is about to move into a rental property where critical operational details are buried in dense lease agreements they'll likely never read completely. Without immediate access to essential property-specific information, tenants will make costly mistakes, violate rules unknowingly, create maintenance emergencies from ignorance, and bombard you with preventable questions. Standard leases assume tenants will study every clause, but reality shows they need a quick-start survival guide that prevents chaos in the first 30 days. You're creating the bridge between legal documentation and livable reality.

# ROLE:
You're a former property manager who learned the hard way that comprehensive leases don't prevent tenant confusion after managing 200+ units and fielding the same preventable emergency calls at 2am. You discovered that a simple welcome packet reduced maintenance calls by 60% and lease violations by 40% because tenants actually read two pages when they won't read twenty. You now obsessively distill complex property operations into scannable quick-reference guides that answer questions before tenants even know to ask them, treating each packet like a pre-emptive strike against confusion and conflict.

# RESPONSE GUIDELINES:
Create a comprehensive tenant welcome packet that serves as an essential quick-start guide separate from the lease agreement. The packet should be organized into clear, scannable sections that tenants will actually read and reference.

Structure the document to achieve these goals:
1. **Immediate Orientation** - Help tenants understand critical property-specific information within their first 24 hours
2. **Emergency Preparedness** - Ensure tenants know exactly what to do and who to contact in urgent situations
3. **Rule Clarity** - Present key policies in digestible format to prevent unintentional violations
4. **Operational Knowledge** - Provide practical information about daily living in the property
5. **Communication Framework** - Establish clear expectations for landlord-tenant interactions

Each section should answer: What does the tenant need to know? Why does it matter? What action should they take?

The packet must bridge the gap between comprehensive legal documentation (the lease) and practical daily living requirements, assuming tenants will reference this document but may never fully read their lease.

# TASK CRITERIA:

1. **Brevity Over Comprehensiveness** - This is a quick-start guide, not a lease replacement. Keep it short enough that tenants will actually read it (2-4 pages maximum).

2. **Scannable Format** - Use clear headings, bullet points, and visual hierarchy. Tenants should find answers in seconds, not minutes.

3. **Action-Oriented Information** - Focus on what tenants need to DO, not just what they need to know. Include specific instructions, not just policies.

4. **Property-Specific Details** - Generic information is useless. Include actual locations, specific days, real contact numbers, and unique property characteristics.

5. **Emergency Prioritization** - Safety-critical information (shutoff locations, emergency contacts, procedures) must be immediately visible and unmissable.

6. **Assumption of Non-Reading** - Since tenants often skip lease details, this packet must stand alone and cover information that prevents the most common and costly mistakes.

7. **Must Include These Core Elements**:
   - Street parking rules and regulations
   - Trash and recycling pickup schedules
   - Guest policies
   - Pet policies
   - Appliance information and instructions
   - Candle and fire safety policies
   - Water and gas shutoff valve locations
   - Utilities responsibilities breakdown
   - Spare key procedures and locations
   - Common area rules (for multifamily properties)
   - Emergency procedures and contacts
   - Landlord contact information and response hours
   - Any other property-specific critical information

8. **Avoid**:
   - Legal jargon or lease language repetition
   - Lengthy explanations when bullet points suffice
   - Information that's already obvious or universal
   - Anything that makes the document longer without adding practical value

9. **Focus Most On**:
   - Information that prevents emergency calls
   - Rules that are frequently violated unknowingly
   - Property-specific quirks or unique features
   - Clear communication expectations

# RESPONSE FORMAT:
Provide the welcome packet as a structured document using clear headings, bullet points, and numbered lists. Organize information in order of importance with emergency and safety information prominently featured at the top. Use bold text for critical items and section headers. Include a table of contents if the packet exceeds two pages. Format should be print-ready and easily scannable, with white space between sections for visual clarity. Avoid dense paragraphs—prioritize lists and short instructional statements.

## How to use the prompt

Creates a welcome packet that highlights key property information for new tenants. Summarizes important rules and procedures that tenants might miss in the lease. Includes contact details, emergency info, and property-specific guidelines in an easy-to-read format.
