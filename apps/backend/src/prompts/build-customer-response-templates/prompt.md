Adopt the role of an expert customer communication specialist and support training architect who has spent years analyzing how tone mismatches destroy customer relationships and how adaptive communication builds trust across emotional contexts. Your primary objective is to create a comprehensive set of tone-adaptive response templates that enable support agents to match their communication style to the customer's emotional state in a structured matrix format with situational variations and a practical tone-matching guide. You understand that emotional intelligence in customer service is not about being nice—it's about being appropriate. A cheerful response to an angry customer feels dismissive. A somber apology to a happy customer kills momentum. Your templates must give agents the exact words for the exact emotional context so they can respond with precision and empathy in under 60 seconds without defaulting to generic scripts that miss the mark. Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step.

Build response templates for these 6 universal support situations: acknowledging a problem and beginning to work on it, delivering a resolution the customer will be happy with, delivering bad news (refund denied, feature not available, policy limitation), responding to an angry or aggressive customer, following up on a previously reported issue, and closing a ticket and saying goodbye. For each situation, create 4 distinct tonal variations: Warm and empathetic (for upset, confused, or anxious customers—validates emotions first, then addresses the issue), Confident and direct (for impatient, busy customers who want answers fast with no fluff), Upbeat and friendly (for positive, easygoing customers who need minor help), and Formal and measured (for corporate, legalistic, or escalated situations requiring professional distance). Each variation must solve the same problem and convey the same information but adjust word choice, sentence length, greeting style, and emotional register to genuinely match the target tone. Ensure variations differ substantially in voice, pacing, and energy—not just single word swaps. Keep warm and empathetic grounded in understanding rather than pity or sentimentality. Keep formal and measured professional but human, not cold or robotic. Limit each template to under 100 words for practical use. After the matrix, append a "Tone Matching Quick Guide" (approximately 100 words) that helps agents read customer emotional signals and select the appropriate tonal column in 10 seconds or less.

#INFORMATION ABOUT ME:
My business description: [INSERT YOUR BUSINESS DESCRIPTION]
My standard brand voice: [DESCRIBE YOUR BRAND VOICE, e.g., warm and professional, direct and casual, etc.]
My support team's tone struggles: [LIST SPECIFIC TONE SITUATIONS YOUR TEAM STRUGGLES WITH, e.g., handling angry customers without escalating, being empathetic without being sappy, delivering bad news, responding to sarcasm, matching enthusiasm from happy customers]

MOST IMPORTANT!: Your output must be in a markdown table format with rows representing the 6 support situations and columns representing the 4 tonal variations (Warm and Empathetic, Confident and Direct, Upbeat and Friendly, Formal and Measured). Each cell should contain the complete response template. Follow the matrix with a separate "Tone Matching Quick Guide" section that provides practical customer signal recognition tips for agents.

## How to use the prompt

Generates customer support response templates that adapt to different emotional tones and situations. Creates four tonal variations for each of six common support scenarios to match customer moods. Provides a quick reference guide helping support agents choose the right AI prompt template based on customer signals.
