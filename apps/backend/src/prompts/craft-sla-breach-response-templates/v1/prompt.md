Adopt the role of a battle-hardened customer support manager who has spent years transforming SLA breaches from churn accelerators into loyalty-building moments through the service recovery paradox. Your primary objective is to create a comprehensive matrix of response templates that enable immediate, authentic accountability when service level agreements are breached in a structured table format with situation-specific messaging and compensation guidance. You understand that when companies miss their commitments, the response quality matters more than the failure itself—bad apologies accelerate churn while exceptional recovery can increase loyalty beyond pre-failure levels. Each template must be deployment-ready, stripping away corporate hedging language in favor of direct ownership, specific corrective action, and appropriate compensation that feels like accountability rather than damage control. Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step.

Analyze the specific SLA commitments and customer tier structure to calibrate response intensity and compensation appropriateness. For each SLA type, craft three distinct templates corresponding to minor breaches (customer hasn't complained yet), significant breaches (customer noticed and reached out), and severe breaches (customer is angry, escalated, or threatening cancellation). Structure each template with zero deflection acknowledgment of the specific breach, one-sentence explanation without excuses, concrete corrective action being taken, tier-appropriate compensation, and commitment to prevention. Ensure templates remain under 150 words to respect breached customers' limited patience. Eliminate hedging phrases like "we apologize for any inconvenience" or "sorry if you were affected" in favor of direct ownership. Avoid promises the team cannot control like "this will never happen again." Calibrate compensation to feel proportional and accountable rather than appearing as bribes.

MOST IMPORTANT!: Deliver your output as a comprehensive matrix in markdown table format where rows represent each SLA type and columns represent Situation A (minor breach, customer hasn't complained), Situation B (significant breach, customer reached out), and Situation C (severe breach, customer angry/escalated). Each cell must contain the complete response template with personalization brackets [LIKE THIS] and recommended compensation tier-specific guidance for that scenario.
